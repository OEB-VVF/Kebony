These rates are currently hardcoded in the Python module. They apply identically to every invoice.

Rate Override at Invoice Level

The default rates above remain as system defaults. However, users will be able to change the rate on each invoice via new editable percentage fields on the invoice header:

FieldDefault ValueEditable?Scope
Marketing Rate (%)4% (wood) / 1.5% (accessory)Yes — user can type any valuePer invoice
Royalty Rate (%)5% (wood) / 0% (accessory)YesPer invoice
Mgmt Fees Rate (%)14% (wood) / 5% (accessory)YesPer invoice

How it works:

  • When a new invoice is created, the rate fields are pre-filled with the default rates based on product classification
  • The user can change the rate to any percentage before posting — for example, set marketing to 2% for a promotional deal, or 7% for a special agreement
  • Setting the rate to 0% disables the accrual (same as the existing on/off toggle, but more granular)
  • The existing on/off checkboxes remain as a quick way to force 0%
  • Once the invoice is posted, the rate is locked (snapshot — cannot be changed retroactively)

Why this change: The hardcoded approach forces the same percentage on every invoice. Business reality requires flexibility — promotional deals, renegotiated terms, special customer agreements, one-off exceptions. The default rates cover 90%+ of invoices automatically; the override handles the exceptions without requiring code changes.

2. Credit Note Accruals

Current Behaviour

Credit notes (out_refund) already generate accruals with a negative sign. When a credit note is posted, accruals are computed using the same rates and rules as a regular invoice, but the amounts are negative — reducing the liability balance.

Example: A credit note of $10,000 on wood with 4% marketing → marketing accrual of −$400.

Enhancement: Rate Override on Credit Notes

With the new rate override fields (Change #1 above), credit notes become fully flexible:

  • The rate fields are available on credit notes, just like on invoices
  • The user can set the rate on the credit note to match the original invoice's accrual rate (e.g., if the original invoice had a special 2% marketing rate, the credit note can also use 2%)
  • Or set a different rate if the commercial terms for the reversal differ
  • This ensures the accrual reversal on the credit note is proportional and accurate

3. Accrual Lifecycle — Permanent Liabilities

Principle: Accruals Live Forever

Once posted, accrual liabilities in accounts 2155xx remain on the balance sheet indefinitely until explicitly reversed by a manual journal entry.

There is no automatic expiry or auto-reversal:

  • ✗ No 90-day auto-reversal
  • ✗ No periodic cleanup job
  • ✗ No scheduled batch to clear old provisions
  • ✓ Liabilities accumulate until the corresponding actual expense arrives (vendor invoice) or the accounting team explicitly releases them

To Release / Reverse a Provision

The accounting team must:

  1. Notify the external accounting firm (Analytix)
  2. Analytix posts a reversal journal entry:
DR  2155xx   Accrual Liability
    CR  780000   Provision Release (Income)
  1. The reversal is documented with a reference to the original accrual period/invoice

Why Permanent?

Revenue-based provisions under ASC 606 / IFRS 15 should remain until settlement. Automatic expiry creates a risk of prematurely releasing liabilities that are still owed — for example:

  • A marketing fund that pays out annually (liability accumulates 12 months before settlement)
  • Royalties settled quarterly (liability sits 3 months)
  • Management fees invoiced with variable delays

The conservative approach: keep the liability until the business explicitly confirms it is no longer owed. This is auditor-friendly and avoids the risk of understating liabilities.
